MATURITY SPECTRUM · EMBODIED & POETIC INTERACTIONS

From Theory to the Mainstream

A Technology Readiness Level (TRL) scale doesn't quite fit a field built on philosophy and art rather than engineering — so here it is reinterpreted as a maturity and adoption spectrum. ~7 min read.

Most emerging technologies in this hub can be placed on a fairly literal TRL scale: lab prototype, pilot, commercial product. Embodied and poetic interaction doesn't move through the world that way. Its ideas often start as philosophy, get tested as one-off art pieces, circulate through galleries and festivals, and only occasionally — and usually stripped of their strangeness — surface in mainstream products. The four stages below track that path instead.

Least Mature

Academic Theory

Ideas exist as philosophy, cognitive science, and critical theory — argued in books and papers, not yet built. This is where embodied cognition, enactivism, and phenomenology live, and where frameworks like 4E cognition and Deleuze and Guattari's concepts of sensation and the Body without Organs originate.

Examples: Merleau-Ponty's Phenomenology of Perception, Varela and Maturana's work on enactivism, Dourish's Where the Action Is.

Emerging

Experimental Art Installations

Theory gets built, once, usually by an artist or a research lab, to test whether an idea holds up when a body actually has to move through it. These pieces are rarely reproduced exactly — each is closer to a working essay than a product.

Examples: student thesis installations, university media-lab prototypes, one-off pieces built for a single festival or exhibition.

Established Practice

Gallery & Exhibition Work

The idea has been refined enough to tour — built to survive repeated installation, different rooms, and audiences who've never met the artist. This is where reputations and vocabularies solidify, and where most of the "artists to know" in this field's field kit operate.

Examples: Rafael Lozano-Hemmer's touring works, Random International's Rain Room, teamLab's exhibitions, Studio Roosegaarde's public commissions.

Most Mature

Mainstream Interactive Media

Fragments of embodied and poetic thinking reach a mass audience — usually simplified, commercialized, and stripped of their original ambiguity in the process. A gesture-based game or a responsive retail display carries a faint trace of the theory it descended from, even if no one involved in building it read the philosophy.

Examples: motion-controlled gaming (Kinect-era titles, VR fitness apps), gesture-based museum kiosks, responsive retail window displays, ambient wellness devices that respond to breath or posture.

Reading the Spectrum

Movement along this spectrum is not always forward or one-directional, and most works never reach level 4 — nor are they trying to. A piece can be extremely mature as art (level 3) while remaining, by design, incompatible with mass-market simplification. That resistance to flattening is often the point: see the Manifesto for the design principles that keep poetic interaction from collapsing into a feature list.
